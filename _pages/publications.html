Working Papers

Health Shocks, Labor Supply, and Spousal Spillovers: Evidence on Job Transitions
Job Market Paper

This paper examines the impact of unexpected health shocks, specifically cancer, stroke, or heart attack, on labor supply adjustments among older workers, as well as spousal spillover effects. Using longitudinal data from the Health and Retirement Study (HRS) from 1992 to 2018 and exploiting variation in the timing of health shocks, the study implements a stacked difference-in-differences framework combined with an event-study design. The results show that individuals experiencing health shocks are 7.9 percentage points less likely to work, 6.8 percentage points more likely to retire, and work 1.5 fewer hours per week. However, there is no evidence of adjustments along other margins, including occupational mobility, changes in job physical demands, or transitions from full-time to part-time work. In contrast, spousal responses are more limited along extensive margins but show meaningful adjustment in job transitions. While the paper finds only a 2.6-percentage point decline in the probability of working among spouses, it finds that spouses are 3.9 percentage points more likely to transition to less physically demanding jobs, which likely reflect caregiving responsibilities. It finds no evidence of spousal adjustments through occupational mobility or transitions from full-time to part-time work. The results underscore the importance of household-level responses to health shocks and have implications for labor supply, caregiving, and social insurance policy.

Keywords: health shocks, labor supply, spousal spillover
JEL Classification: I12, J22, J14, J12
